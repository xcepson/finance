In retail banking, when a loan stops performing as expected, the focus shifts from relationship management to risk mitigation. This document outlines the comprehensive framework for the recovery of retail loans, detailing everything from early warning mechanisms to legal enforcement.

1. What is Recovery of Retail Loans?

Recovery of retail loans refers to the systematic process employed by a bank to collect the principal and interest owed on a loan that has passed its due date. It is the primary mechanism banks use to protect their capital and maintain liquidity. The process spans a wide spectrum—from gentle SMS reminders for a user who simply forgot to pay, all the way to legal repossession of physical assets when a borrower willfully refuses to pay.

2. Default vs. Re-scheduling

These are two distinct outcomes when a borrower faces financial stress:

- Default: This occurs when a borrower violates the legal terms of the loan agreement, most commonly by failing to make the Equated Monthly Installments (EMIs) on time. Default is a breach of contract that triggers the bank's recovery mechanisms and significantly damages the borrower's credit score.
    
- Re-scheduling (Restructuring): This is a proactive alternative to default. If a borrower faces genuine hardship (e.g., job loss or medical emergency), the bank may agree to alter the original terms of the loan to make it affordable again. This usually involves extending the loan tenure to lower the EMI, temporarily reducing the interest rate, or granting a moratorium (a pause on payments).
    

3. Monitoring of Loan Accounts

Banks do not wait for a loan to default before taking action; they utilize advanced analytics to monitor accounts for Early Warning Signals (EWS). Effective monitoring includes tracking:

- Payment behavior: Bouncing of NACH mandates, post-dated cheques, or consistently paying EMIs on the last possible grace day.
    
- Operating account health: Sudden, sustained drops in salary credits, frequent overdrafts, or maintaining near-zero balances in primary savings accounts.
    
- External signals: Drops in the borrower's external credit score (such as CIBIL) or defaults on credit cards held with other banking institutions.
    

When EWS triggers are hit, the bank's system automatically flags the account for early intervention, often prompting a call from the relationship manager to understand the borrower's situation.

4. Classification of Irregular Loan Accounts

In India, the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) mandates a strict, day-by-day classification system for irregular accounts. As an account ages past its due date, it moves through specific risk buckets requiring increasing provisions (capital the bank must set aside to cover the potential loss).

Pre-NPA: Special Mention Accounts (SMA)

Before a loan is officially classified as bad, it is flagged as an SMA to ensure the bank acts quickly.

|   |   |   |
|---|---|---|
|Classification|Days Overdue|Bank's Action|
|SMA - 0|1 to 30 days|Soft reminders via SMS, email, and automated calls.|
|SMA - 1|31 to 60 days|Tele-calling team engages; formal notice may be sent.|
|SMA - 2|61 to 90 days|Field visits initiated; final warning before severe legal action.|

  

Non-Performing Assets (NPA)

If a loan remains unpaid for more than 90 days, it is officially classified as an NPA, severely restricting the borrower's financial future.

|   |   |   |
|---|---|---|
|Classification|NPA Duration|Risk Status|
|Sub-standard Asset|Up to 12 months|Bank expects some loss; must provision 15-25% of the loan value.|
|Doubtful Asset|More than 12 months|Recovery is highly questionable; heavy provisioning required.|
|Loss Asset|Identified as uncollectible|The asset is written off the bank's active balance sheet, though recovery efforts may continue in the background.|

  

5. The Recovery Policy of Banks

Every bank must have a Board-approved recovery policy adhering to the RBI's Fair Practices Code. The overarching philosophy is that recovery should be firm but ethical.

- Customer Dignity: Agents cannot use abusive language, physical threats, or public humiliation.
    
- Time Boundaries: Recovery calls and visits are strictly restricted to standard hours (typically 7:00 AM to 7:00 PM in India).
    
- Data Privacy: Agents cannot discuss the borrower's debt with neighbors, employers, or extended family members.
    
- One-Time Settlement (OTS): In late-stage recovery, if a borrower has intent but no capacity to pay the full amount, the bank's policy may allow for an OTS. The bank accepts a "haircut" (a lump sum lower than the total due) to close the account and avoid drawn-out legal costs.
    

6. The SARFAESI Act

The Securitisation and Reconstruction of Financial Assets and Enforcement of Security Interest (SARFAESI) Act, 2002 is one of the most powerful tools in an Indian retail bank's arsenal.

It allows banks to auction residential or commercial properties used as collateral to recover a loan without the intervention of a court.

Important Constraint: SARFAESI only applies to secured loans (like Home Loans or Loans Against Property) where the outstanding NPA balance is over ₹1 Lakh and accounts for more than 20% of the principal and interest. It cannot be used for unsecured personal loans or credit cards.

The Strict Timeline for SARFAESI Invocation:

- Day 0 (Account becomes NPA): The borrower's loan is officially classified as a Non-Performing Asset after 90 days of non-payment.
    
- Day 1 (Section 13(2) Demand Notice): The bank issues a formal notice demanding the borrower clear the entire outstanding due within 60 days. The borrower has the right to send a written objection, which the bank must reply to within 15 days.
    
- Day 61 (Section 13(4) Possession): If the dues are not cleared, the bank has the legal right to take "symbolic" or physical possession of the property. They may change the locks and place a repossession notice on the door.
    
- Day 90+ (Auction Notice): Before selling the property, the bank must publish an auction notice in at least two leading newspapers, giving the borrower a final 30-day window to pay before the public auction.
