In retail banking and wealth management, payment cards are essential tools for consumer liquidity, transaction processing, and credit management. This document provides a comprehensive overview of the primary payment card types and technologies used across the industry.

1. What are Credit Cards?

A credit card is a payment instrument issued by a financial institution that provides the cardholder with a revolving line of credit. Instead of deducting money directly from a bank account, the issuing bank pays the merchant on your behalf, and you repay the bank at a later date.

·        **Mechanism:** You are assigned a credit limit based on your income, credit history, and internal risk assessment.

·        **Grace Period:** An interest-free window (typically 20 to 50 days) between the transaction date and the billing due date.

·        **Flexibility:** Cardholders can choose to pay the statement balance in full without incurring interest, or pay a minimum amount and carry over (revolve) the remaining balance while accruing interest.

2. What are Charge Cards?

A charge card looks and functions like a credit card at the point of sale, but it has one fundamental operational difference: it does not offer a revolving line of credit.

·        **Full Payment Mandate:** Cardholders are required to pay the full statement balance every single month by the due date.

·        **No Standard Interest Rates:** Because carrying a balance is not permitted, charge cards do not charge standard APR/interest rates. However, late or missed payments trigger heavy fees and immediate card suspension.

·        **No Preset Spending Limit:** Instead of a fixed credit limit, purchasing capacity adjusts dynamically based on the customer's spending habits, payment history, and overall financial standing (e.g., traditional American Express charge cards).

3. What are Prepaid Cards?

A prepaid card is a stored-value card where funds must be loaded into the card account before any transactions can be processed.

·        **No Bank Account Required:** Prepaid cards function independently of bank savings or checking accounts and do not involve extending debt.

·        **Spending Limit:** Cardholders can only spend up to the pre-loaded amount. Once the balance reaches zero, the card cannot be used until funds are reloaded.

·        **Common Applications:** Forex/travel cards (pre-loaded with foreign currencies), gift cards, corporate expense cards, and financial inclusion tools for unbanked populations.

4. What are Debit Cards?

A debit card is a payment card linked directly to a cardholder's bank account, such as a Savings Account or Current Account.

·        **Immediate Settlement:** When a purchase or ATM withdrawal is executed, funds are debited directly and immediately from the customer's available bank balance.

·        **Debt Control:** Customers cannot spend more money than what is available in their linked account, unless an approved overdraft facility is in place.

·        **Dual Functionality:** Serves both as an ATM card for cash withdrawals and deposits, and as a payment instrument across Point-of-Sale (POS) terminals and e-commerce platforms.

5. What are Co-Branded Cards?

A co-branded card is a credit or debit card issued through a strategic partnership between a bank (card issuer) and a retail brand, airline, or merchant enterprise.

6. What are Contactless Cards?

A contactless card is equipped with Near Field Communication (NFC) and Radio-Frequency Identification (RFID) technology, enabling "tap-to-pay" transactions.

·        **Tap-to-Pay Mechanism:** Instead of swiping or inserting the card into a reader, the user holds the card within a few centimeters of an NFC-enabled POS terminal.

·        **Speed & Convenience:** Transactions complete in seconds without physical contact or wear-and-tear on the card's magnetic stripe or EMV chip.

·        **PIN Threshold Rules:** Regulatory mandates permit PIN-less payments up to a defined threshold (e.g., up to ₹5,000 per transaction in India). Purchases above this threshold require PIN entry for enhanced security.

·        **Cryptographic Security:** Each contactless transaction generates a unique, dynamic one-time code (tokenization), preventing data theft and card skimming.
